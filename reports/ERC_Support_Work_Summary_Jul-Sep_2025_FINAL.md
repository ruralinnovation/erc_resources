# Economic Recovery Corps (ERC) Support Work Summary
## July - September 2025

---

## Executive Summary

During the third quarter of 2025, the Mapping & Data Analytics team at the Center on Rural Innovation (CORI) advanced the Economic Recovery Corps (ERC) program through two primary initiatives: comprehensive scoping of an innovative Network Mapping tool designed to visualize ERC fellow networks and partnerships, and strategic collaboration with Will Warren on his ERC capstone project focused on economic development coordination across Eastern Kentucky. This period established the technical and analytical foundation for transforming ERC's network data into actionable intelligence for measuring collaborative impact and network effects in rural economic development. Concurrently, CORI formalized and communicated the technical approach, budget, and governance framework for the Network Mapping tool with external stakeholders at the International Economic Development Council (IEDC).

## 1. Economic Recovery Corps (ERC) Network Mapping Tool Scoping

### Overview and Context

The ERC program trains fellows—emerging leaders committed to economic development in rural communities—while building strategic partnerships with organizations focused on innovation and economic change. Historically, the program captured partnership data in quarterly reports but lacked a systematic approach to visualizing network structures, understanding relationship strength, and measuring collaborative outcomes. The Network Mapping tool initiative aims to transform raw quarterly data into interactive network visualizations and strategic insights while addressing critical gaps in collaborative impact attribution.

### Initial Data Analysis and Gap Identification (July 2025)

Beginning in early July 2025, a comprehensive analysis of existing ERC quarterly report data structures was conducted. This analysis examined Q1 2025 quarterly report data from four fellows: Beverly Lee (Tullahoma, TN region), Erika Haskins (Maine), Gabrielle Cash (Virginia/Shenandoah region), and Will Warren (Kentucky/Appalachian region).

**Current Data Baseline:**
- **Total partnerships documented**: 21
- **Unique partner organizations**: 21 (zero overlap between fellows)
- **Existing partnerships**: 11 (52% of documented relationships)
- **New partnerships**: 6 (29% of relationships)
- **Partnership relationship types**: Host/Existing/New classification
- **Network density**: 0.07 (extremely sparse—indicating complete isolation between fellows)
- **Network structure**: Hub-and-spoke only (no cross-fellow connections)

**Data Collection Strengths:**
The current quarterly report structure successfully captures:
- Organization names and missions (100% documented)
- Collaboration descriptions and relationship context (documented)
- Distinction between host organizations, existing, and new partnerships
- Basic partnership categorization by relationship type
- Geographic and sectoral diversity (21 organizations span diverse sectors and regions)

### Critical Gaps Identified in Data Structure

Analysis conducted by July 17, 2025 revealed **four major structural limitations** preventing effective network mapping and collaborative impact assessment:

#### Gap 1: Collaborative Impact Attribution Deficits

**Problem:** The current structure cannot demonstrate network effects or collaborative amplification.

**Specific Issues:**

- **Collaborative vs. Individual Impact Attribution**: Current funding and outcome data exists in isolation from partnership information. Will Warren reports 8 partnerships but no mechanism exists to determine which partnerships jointly contributed to specific outcomes vs. individual relationships. Cannot distinguish between outcomes from multi-stakeholder collaboration vs. individual fellow efforts.

- **Multi-Stakeholder Project Identification**: Current data lists partnerships separately but doesn't identify which ones are working together on the same initiative. Example: Beverly Lee reports partnerships with both "Coffee County Industrial Board" and "Tennessee Economic and Community Development"—but no data shows whether these represent separate relationships or multi-stakeholder participation in a single project.

- **Stakeholder Type Classification Missing**: Current data has organization names but no systematic stakeholder type categorization. Cannot determine if a project involves active participation from two or more distinct types of stakeholder groups.

**Strategic Impact**: This limitation is particularly significant given that the 21 partnerships span diverse sectors and geographic regions, suggesting natural collaboration opportunities that remain invisible in current data.

#### Gap 2: Data Quality and Standardization Issues

**Problem:** Partner name standardization challenges undermine reliable network analysis.

**Specific Issues:**
- No standardized organization identifiers to ensure consistent tracking
- Free-text entry allowing duplicate organizations under different names
- No systematic approach to organization categorization beyond relationship type
- Zero shared partnerships across all 21 organizations despite diversity suggesting natural connections

#### Gap 3: Missing Relationship Context and Strength Indicators

**Problem:** Current partnership documentation lacks critical relationship attributes needed for prioritization and impact assessment.

**Specific Issues:**
- **No relationship strength measurement**: All partnerships appear equivalent regardless of engagement intensity. Will Warren's 8 partnerships and Gabrielle Cash's 2 partnerships appear identical in importance.
- **No temporal tracking**: Partnership evolution and duration are invisible
- **No role specification**: Fellow influence and leadership within partnerships undefined
- **No outcome attribution**: Specific impacts from individual partnerships cannot be isolated

**Consequence**: The 11 "existing partnerships" and 6 "new partnerships" appear identical in importance and impact, masking the strategic value of different collaboration types.

#### Gap 4: Network Isolation and Connectivity Gaps

**Problem:** Complete network isolation between fellows prevents understanding cross-fellow collaboration and learning.

**Analysis Findings:**
- **Zero shared partnerships** across all 21 organizations
- **Network density of 0.07** — indicating extremely sparse connectivity
- **Hub-and-spoke structure only** — no cross-fellow connections detected
- **21 unique organizations with no overlap** between fellow ecosystems
- **Unequal engagement visibility**: Will Warren operates with 8 partnerships while Gabrielle Cash has only 2, yet no data exists to indicate whether knowledge sharing or collaboration occurs between different engagement levels

**Strategic Question**: This isolation pattern suggests that either (a) genuine collaborations between fellows are not being captured, or (b) opportunities for strategic network development are being missed.

### Data-Driven Enhancement Roadmap

Based on this detailed analysis of the actual data structure and identified gaps, four priority enhancements were recommended to address collaborative impact attribution and network mapping requirements:

#### Priority 1: Stakeholder Type & Cross-Partner Collaboration Tracking

**Objective**: Enable identification of multi-stakeholder collaboration and stakeholder diversity within initiatives

**Required Additions** (+2 minutes per partnership completion time):
- Dropdown selection of organization type categorization (Government/Private/Nonprofit/Academic/Federal)
- Dropdown selection of geographic scope (Local/Regional/State/National)
- Optional: Dropdown selection from organization registry for frequent partners
- "Do any of your partnerships work together on shared initiatives?"
- If yes: "Which organizations collaborate with each other (not just with you)?"
- For each outcome/funding success: "Which partnerships contributed to this result?"
- "Was this achieved through multi-stakeholder collaboration or individual partnership?"

**Rationale**: These additions would enable automatic calculation of stakeholder diversity within projects, as well as surface collaborative connections between partners that may have direct relevance to impact and outcome measures.

**Strategic Value**: High—enables network visualization and collaborative impact identification

#### Priority 2: Relationship Strength and Engagement Metrics

**Objective**: Enable prioritization of high-impact partnerships and identification of underutilized network connections

**Required Additions** (+0.5 minutes per partnership completion time):
- Meeting frequency dropdown for each partnership (Monthly/Quarterly/As-needed/One-time)
- Role specification (Lead/Co-lead/Contributor/Advisor/Observer)
- Initiative type (Ongoing collaboration/Project-specific/Advisory/Funding-related)

**Rationale**: Current data treats Will Warren's 8 partnerships and Gabrielle Cash's 2 partnerships as equivalent. Relationship strength data would enable prioritization of high-impact partnerships and identification of underutilized network connections.

**Strategic Value**: Very High—distinguishes active collaborations from peripheral connections

#### Priority 3: Temporal Relationship Tracking

**Objective**: Enable understanding of partnership evolution and strategic timing of collaborations

**Required Additions** (+1 minute per partnership completion time):
- Partnership start date (month/year)
- Expected duration or next milestone
- Partnership status (Active/Completed/On-hold/Planned)

**Rationale**: Network mapping requires understanding relationship evolution. Current data provides no insight into partnership development patterns or strategic timing of collaborations.

**Strategic Value**: Medium—enables longitudinal network analysis and relationship lifecycle tracking

#### Priority 4: Outcome Attribution and Impact Linkage

**Objective**: Link partnership relationships to measured economic development outcomes, demonstrating network value

**Required Additions** (+1.5 minutes per outcome completion time):
- Specific outcomes attributable to each partnership (checklist format)
- Collaborative achievements requiring multiple organizations
- Resource leveraging or cost-sharing arrangements
- Policy or strategic plan influences

**Rationale**: Current funding and outcome data exists in isolation from partnership information. Linking outcomes to specific relationships would demonstrate network value and partnership ROI.

**Strategic Value**: Very High—enables impact demonstration and outcome attribution

## 2. Strategic Collaboration with Will Warren on ERC Capstone Project

### Overview and Context

Will Warren, serving as an Economic Recovery Corps Fellow based in Eastern Kentucky with host organization Shaping Our Appalachian Region (SOAR), engaged the CORI Mapping & Data Analytics team for technical assistance and planning support on his capstone project. Will's capstone addresses a critical gap in regional economic development coordination: the lack of unified strategic planning and comparative analysis across the eight Area Development Districts (ADDs) that serve the 54 Appalachian counties in Kentucky.

### Will Warren's Capstone Project: Outreach and Gap Analysis

**Project Scope:**
Will Warren's capstone project focuses on three interconnected work streams:

1. **Outreach and Action Research**
   - Direct engagement with the 8 area development regions that serve Eastern Kentucky
   - Building relationships and understanding current economic development practices
   - Gathering Strategic Economic Development (SEDS) plans and Comprehensive Economic Development Strategies (CEDS)

2. **Gap Analysis on SEDs/CEDS**
   - Analyzing each ADD's five-year strategic development plans
   - Identifying parallels and gaps across the region
   - Understanding where overlap and duplication exist
   - Identifying missing elements that should be included

3. **Platform Development**
   - Building a platform to identify and match projects across service areas with appropriate funding sources
   - Creating a "matchmaker" system connecting grant and philanthropic funding with regional economic development projects
   - Addressing coordination challenges that currently prevent resources from being efficiently deployed

**Strategic Objective:**
Will's work aims to help the eight ADDs become more unified as a region by:
- Better understanding what each organization is doing
- Identifying missing capabilities and redundancies
- Creating communication tools to support regional coordination
- Building unified regional strategy while respecting local autonomy

### CORI Technical Assistance and Collaboration

#### Initial Engagement (Late June - Early July 2025)

The CORI Mapping & Data Analytics team received initial outreach from Will Warren regarding technical assistance needs for his capstone project. Initial meeting occurred on **July 2, 2025**, where John Hall and Will Warren discussed potential collaboration areas.

**Identified Areas for Technical Support:**

1. **Technical Consulting & Platform Development**
   - Platform development guidance with regards to deploying and managing a bespoke social network via Mighty Networks
   - Technical architecture recommendations for managing data across multiple service areas and development regions
   - Integration support for grant and philanthropic funding databases

2. **Data Analysis & Research Support**
   - Assistance with gap analysis on Strategic Economic Development (SEDS) plans across the 8 area development regions
   - Data consolidation and analysis support for the various CEDS reports being collected
   - Research methodology guidance for action research and SEDS process work

3. **Implementation Strategy**
   - Impact measurement framework development for tracking successful project-funding matches
   - Technical setup for monitoring platform usage and outcomes across regions
   - General project planning and execution support

#### CEDS Analysis and Feedback (July-August 2025)

**Materials Provided by Will Warren:**
Will Warren provided comprehensive documentation including:
- Eight CEDS reports from Kentucky Area Development Districts (5-year strategic plans)
- Comparative examples including a Tri-State CEDS Comparison Report (Massachusetts, Vermont, New Hampshire)
- Detailed documentation of CEDS requirements and structure
- Draft article on rural workforce development issues in Appalachia

**CORI Analysis:**
The CORI data team, led by Lindsey Elliott and John Hall, conducted detailed analysis of the CEDS materials provided, identifying:

**Key Findings on CEDS Reports:**

1. **Data Availability and Quality Issues**
   - While each ADD defines Goals and Objectives clearly, they do not document completion or achievement
   - No specific impact data on ADD activities (e.g., how many employees staffed, jobs created)
   - County-level data comes from Census Bureau but does not reflect ADD-specific contributions
   - Significant gap: No information on how much ADDs have directly supported economic outcomes

2. **Structural Inconsistencies**
   - CEDS reports vary dramatically in length and format (ranging from ~30 to 74 pages)
   - Different approaches to data presentation and strategy organization
   - Inconsistent metrics and performance measurement frameworks

3. **Content Gaps**
   - Lack of "impact" metrics specific to workforce development across all ADDs
   - Missing linkages between regional goals and actual implementation
   - Limited information on cross-ADD collaboration and shared initiatives

4. **Opportunities for Qualitative Analysis**
   - While quantitative analysis is limited by data gaps, qualitative analysis is very feasible
   - CEDS documents provide excellent starting point for identifying shared goals and strategies
   - Potential to create comparative brief analyzing goals, objectives, and trends across regions
   - Opportunity to use CEDS analysis as communication tool to encourage ADD collaboration

**Strategic Recommendation from CORI:**
Rather than attempting quantitative impact assessment (which would require new data collection from ADDs), a qualitative/narrative analysis would be more productive. This could:
- Identify shared Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) across all ADDs
- Describe general findings and trends in regional economic development approaches
- Highlight underserved vulnerable populations and opportunities for coordinated response
- Demonstrate how regional coordination could address common challenges
- Potentially incentivize ADDs to participate by showing efficiency benefits of collaboration

## 3. External Stakeholder Coordination and Technical Governance

### IEDC Engagement and Technical Approach Formalization (July 31, 2025)

On July 31, 2025, Leigh Anne Albert (CORI) responded formally to Darnesha Tabor (Monitoring and Evaluation Specialist at IEDC, the parent organization of the ERC program) regarding the Network Mapping project proposal and technical approach. This communication formalized the technical architecture, budget, timeline, and use cases for the tool.

#### Technical Stack and Integration Strategy

**Technology Selection:**
- **Platform**: Custom development using R, React, and AWS Cloud infrastructure
- **Rationale**: Selected for performance, flexibility, and long-term maintainability; aligns with CORI's standard analytics project infrastructure
- **Integration Approach**: Lightweight, hosted application embedded on ERC website via iframe or custom WordPress bloc; seamless access with no major coding required
- **Data Collection**: Collection through Airtable feasible but requires "significant overhaul" in capture approach to ensure streamlined process

**Alternative Platform Consideration:**
CORI indicated openness to exploring Kumu or Gephi (open source alternatives) as viable options:
- Effort likely similar—bulk of work is in designing data structure and building visual logic, not coding itself
- Regardless of platform selected, CORI can provide documentation and training to support internal IEDC ownership post-setup if post-grant maintenance contract not desired

#### Budget and Resource Allocation

**Initial Buildout Cost:**
- **Estimated effort**: 520 to 570 hours
- **Status**: Falls within existing ERC data tools grant budget
- **Scope**: All four phases included—user discovery, data collection tools, MVP development, and final version with content rollout
- **Additional funding**: No additional funding required to deliver scoped solution

**Ongoing Maintenance and Support:**
- **During grant term**: Minimal ongoing maintenance (10-15 hours per quarter), assuming streamlined and consistent data collection process
- **Support model**: CORI can provide support through grant term within existing budget
- **Post-grant options**:
  - Option 1: Technical handoff and training for IEDC team to take on hosting and maintenance
  - Option 2: Separate maintenance agreement estimated at 50-100 hours annually (depending on data update frequency)
  - More accurate post-grant estimate possible once map and data structure finalized

#### Primary Use Cases and Strategic Value

**Tool's Strategic Purpose:**
The Network Mapping tool is intended to support:
1. **Visualization of network relationships and patterns of activity** to show impact and scope of network building
2. **Storytelling to funders and stakeholders** about ERC program's network development impact
3. **Identification of collaboration patterns** across fellows and host organizations
4. **Surface areas where network activity has or has not translated into outcomes** — enabling strategic assessment of network effectiveness

**Data Update Strategy:**
Once streamlined data process is in place, updates can be made with minimal effort and with limited or no ongoing developer support.

#### Tool Adoption Status and Challenges (as of July 31, 2025)

**Current Adoption Metrics:**
- **Status**: Adoption slower than expected
- **Early barriers**: Limited fellow access for onboarding (competing demands); significant variability in fellows' data needs
- **Technical issues**: Not technical—open access intentionally left to reduce login friction
- **Usage tracking limitation**: Cannot track individual-level usage due to open access model
- **Positive indicator**: Overall logon activity increasing
- **Support utilization**: Few fellows taking advantage of office hours for one-on-one support

**Promotion and Engagement Activities:**
- Additional demo sessions implemented
- June conference unable to accommodate further demo session
- Need for additional focus group identified
- Potential IEDC support needed: help coordinate deepdive demo with IEDC staff; promotion through Circle platform (ERC's collaboration tool)

**Development Pause:**
- No additional development time allocated toward tool pending further input on usage and value
